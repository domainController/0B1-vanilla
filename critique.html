Clergy of Taste

This page gathers interconnected reflections on food, taste, hierarchy, and the theatrical logic of modern dining. It is the umbrella that connects all critiques on social dinners, culinary rituals, and symbolic performances.

🍇 Epicureanism

We were told civilization began with men who knew how to talk, drink, and judge olives. They legislated from lounges. They drew triangles and declared the laws of nature — by deep intuition. So let’s start there — with Greece, taste, and Epicurus.

According to Epicurus, there are three types of desires. The first are natural and necessary desires: drinking when thirsty, eating when hungry. These are vital needs, shared by both animals and humans. Ignoring them puts life at risk.

The second category is natural but non-necessary desires: sexuality, gustatory pleasures, superfluous cravings. These are shared with animals, but not essential for survival.

Finally, there are non-natural and non-necessary desires: wealth, status, fame. No animal seeks honor or accumulation. These are uniquely human and bring stress, envy, and illusion.

For Epicurus, real pleasure comes only from the satisfaction of natural and necessary desires: drink water, eat bread when needed. Anything else is drift, social theatre, or distraction.

🧭 Value Hierarchy

Every human action reveals a system of values, whether we’re concious of it or not. Working the weekend, skipping a family dinner, visiting friends instead of building a project — each act reflects a priority structure.

We operate inside a value hierarchy. Working to feed your children → survival. Clubbing despite debt → instant pleasure. Choosing stability over discomfort → safety. Each reveals a hidden system of priorities, even when denied or unconscious.

We all exist within layered value systems. The more fragmented, the more we act through contradictory subpersonalities. Wanting to work, yet wasting time. Wanting peace, yet escaping every night. That’s not paradox — that’s confusion of priority.

“Enjoy your life” has become a dominant social injunction, framing life around pleasure, diversion, self-indulgence. We act less by purpose — and more by mood.

🧠 Hédonisme

Aujourd’hui, le plaisir est devenu une fin en soi, non un bonus. Il faut profiter, jouir, vivre. Ne pas le faire est vu comme une faute — un manque d’adaptation, de santé, de désir.

Les slogans ne masquent rien : “Enjoy your life”, “Treat yourself”, “Work hard, play harder”. Ce ne sont pas des conseils : ce sont des injonctions à l’hédonisme social.

Refuser la jouissance, c’est paraître frustré, triste ou malade. Sortir, se faire plaisir, dépenser sont devenus des preuves d’équilibre dans le monde contemporain.

On ne mange plus pour vivre, on vit pour éprouver des sensations. L’assiette devient le théâtre d’un désir non naturel et non nécessaire, au sens d’Épicure : la faim est absente, seule reste la recherche du plaisir.

Ce glissement signe une décadence invisible mais généralisée : les besoins primaires deviennent des objets culturels, codifiés, ritualisés. Il faut apprendre à « bien » manger, imiter les gestes du raffinement, singer l’instinct sous des couches de représentation.

Ce n’est plus le corps qui commande, c’est le regard social. Même la fourchette devient un instrument de conformité, la gastronomie une performance sociale. Le plaisir se dissocie du besoin. L’humain se distingue de l’animal par sa capacité à déformer ses instincts.

Social Dinners

Social dinners are staged meals — status signaling over nutrition. Restaurants become arenas for aesthetics, not nourishment. We celebrate plating, but ignore the body that digests it.

Yet, as early as Hippocrates, food was medicine. The gut — our second brain — sends cravings, alerts, balances. Listening to it means tracking fatigue, tension, hunger type. It's not taste, it's feedback.

We need a well-being restaurant. One that serves functional dishes — based on inflammation levels, sleep quality, or hormonal needs. A place that understands aging isn’t a curse, but a call to precision.

Beyond that, a true restaurant for athletes: upload blood tests, food journals, recovery logs. An AI calculates the exact macros, adjusts portions, balances spice, sugar, salt. Health becomes haute cuisine.

Status Signaling

Status Signaling has become a silent law in modern restaurants. What you eat, where you sit, and who you tag say more than a résumé. You no longer dine—you broadcast.

The emergence of the “foodie” figure—curator of taste, chronicler of plates—turned meals into performances. To be seen eating became as important as eating well. The restaurant became a stage.

Then came foodporn. Perfect lighting. Close-ups of melting yolks. Stylized excess shared on Instagram. Aesthetic jouissance replaced hunger. Visual pleasure replaced nutritional need.

Eating out now signals cultural capital. A reservation at the right address becomes proof of belonging. Each post says: I can afford this, I know this, I deserve this. It’s no longer about taste—it’s about status.

Simulacre des besoins naturels. Managers and waiters often unconsciously align themselves with their clientele, mimicking the attitudes, speech, and gestures of the upper middle class. This creates distance and even disdain toward kitchen or back-office staff. Class division becomes internalized.

Through repeated exposure to wealthier customers, there is a gradual psychological assimilation. The dishwasher is no longer a colleague — they become a social mirror to reject.

Simulacre et Représentation

The restaurant is no longer a place to eat. It is a simulacrum—a theatrical representation of ancestral necessity. The need for food is real. But the form it takes is entirely fabricated.

Each element is staged—the lighting, the plating, the scripted smiles. We don’t eat to live, we rehearse the idea of dining. It’s consumption as narrative, not nutrition.

We simulate ancient rituals (table, fire, sharing) but wrapped in branding and spectacle. The dish is no longer a means—it is a message. A plate now speaks: you belong to a story.

Even hunger becomes optional. The experience matters more than the need. We consume images, we perform desire, and the restaurant becomes a representation of itself—an echo without origin.

Le Divertissement Pascalien

Diversion is the hidden function of contemporary leisure. Echoing Blaise Pascal, the restaurant offers escape from oneself. To eat out is to forget. To fill the void.

It’s not only distraction. It’s pretext. A reason to avoid solitude. Smalltalk becomes the main dish. Glasses of wine hide existential nausea. It’s a masked flight from facing silence.

Alcohol, noise, screens—every tool is used to push away the real. The restaurant becomes a refuge from interiority. We digest bread, but what we hunger for is meaning.

In this ecosystem, even boredom becomes unthinkable. Every evening must be filled. Every discomfort evaded. The restaurant is no longer a destination—it’s a loop of self-distraction.

Restaurant as Collective Ritual

The restaurant is not merely a place to eat. It is a ritualistic outing that holds sacred value, particularly in bourgeois urban environments. Dining out becomes an act of identity performance — a scheduled escape, a celebration of hedonism/epicurian, a confirmation of one’s status.

It offers temporary relief from existential suffering. It is less about food and more about social roles, recognition, and belonging. Alcohol plays a central role in this escape, often forming 50% of the budget. The idea of dining without drinking is almost taboo — alcohol is a crucial anesthetic.

Dining out has become a ritualized expression of collective belonging. It is no longer solely about nourishment but about participating in a staged atmosphere. The sounds of clinking glasses, bustling conversations, and background music construct a communal ambience that reassures participants of their social presence. People do not merely seek food—they seek the experience of being surrounded by others doing the same thing: eating, laughing, and performing happiness. This ambient validation is often more important than the quality of the meal itself.

The restaurant becomes a theater of lifestyle. Choosing a venue is often about its popularity, aesthetics, or social media appeal. Customers go where others go, forming a herd dynamic where recognition, not taste, drives decision-making. The ritual lies in conformity: in being seen in the right place, with the right people, performing the right joy.

Alcohol as Existential Escape

Alcohol is both a social lubricant and an existential escape. It justifies conversation, laughter, silence — even absence. To drink is to forget. To dine is to drink. This dynamic is deeply embedded in restaurant culture and explains much of its emotional charge.

The Culture of Small Talk

Restaurants are arenas of small talk. The loud music, vibrant setting, and crowded ambiance are not incidental—they are intentional tools to prevent silence and suppress depth. Real conversation is drowned by laughter, playlists, and the euphoric buzz of alcohol. What is encouraged is a type of celebratory superficiality. No one goes to restaurants to confront existential despair or speak of emotional wounds; the atmosphere makes such expression socially inappropriate.

Instead, one exchanges compliments, comments on dishes, shares brief anecdotes, and laughs. It is an anesthetic ritual. Dining out is less about cuisine and more about simulating joy. In this way, food becomes a prop, and the restaurant, a soundproof container where avoidance is elevated to a form of social grace.

Culinary Mythologies and Masculine Prestige

There is a persistent mythos surrounding male chefs — as if their creative genius justifies their authority and harshness. The figure of the ‘celebrity chef’ eclipses everyday kitchen realities, where burnout, low wages, and hierarchy dominate.

Female chefs remain rare. The system subtly enshrines masculine command and culinary heroism, despite the irony that many chefs barely cook for themselves at home.

Processed Food and Hedonic Environments

Food quality is rarely the true focus. Unless priced outrageously, meals are usually composed of low-process, cost-optimized ingredients. The customer doesn’t truly seek nutrition — what matters is ambiance, music, aesthetics, and the opportunity for curated joy.

Restaurants offer small talk and status confirmation. They function as collective opiates, not nutritional institutions.

Mimetisme Social

Theorie de Rene Girard. théorie de René Girard (imitation, désir triangulaire). Waiters and managers often unconsciously identify with the clientele, especially when the clientele is affluent. This identification with the bourgeois class creates a perceptible hierarchy inside the restaurant itself: dishwashers and kitchen staff are silently deemed inferior. The front-of-house staff perform a form of class mimicry, adopting gestures, speech, and attitudes that align with those they serve. The more refined their demeanor, the more credible the illusion of upward mobility.

For owners and managers—often upwardly mobile immigrants or self-made entrepreneurs—there is both proximity to wealth and a permanent sense of being outside it. This liminal status often manifests as exaggerated hospitality or covert resentment. It reflects a fragile self-perception: the desire to belong to the elite while knowing one is not entirely accepted within it. This tension trickles down into how back-of-house staff are treated, judged not only by their performance but by their inability—or refusal—to perform social codes.

Cognitive Dissonance and Existential Anesthesia

The modern restaurant embodies a deep contradiction: it is a temple of pleasure built on a foundation of denial. Customers engage in enthusiastic consumption not to celebrate life, but to escape its weight. The ritual hides a void. Alcohol plays a central role—it is the permission slip for euphoria, a socially accepted drug to blur existential anxiety. Without alcohol, much of the ritual collapses: it is the medium through which silence is replaced, burdens are paused, and confidence is artificially inflated.

Underneath the curated aesthetics lies a dissonance: the more luxurious the atmosphere, the more invisible the labor, the deeper the denial. The restaurant becomes a mirror of societal illusion: identity performed through consumption, and meaning drowned in ambiance. It is not just a place to eat—it is a place to pretend.
