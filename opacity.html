⚖️ Visibility & Power Dilution

It took days — not because it was complex, but because I was new.

At the time, I had no background in legal structures or group architecture. It took me two or three days to understand the basics — to read the right registries, spot the holding pattern, and trace prior incidents.

But looking back, it should have taken five minutes. The system was not protected, not layered, not concealed. The dilution of responsibility was fully public — just unread.

What I show here doesn’t prove any skill. It just shows what becomes visible once you know what to check. The gap was not technical — it was cultural and unclaimed.

Layer 1 – Decision Without Clear Ownership

Before the interview, I was not told who I would be meeting. No name, no title, no structure was announced. When I arrived on site, I greeted employees I had briefly met during the trial shift the day before.

A woman I had never seen — later known as Sahar — came up to me directly and greeted me. I assumed she was a senior waitress. Her approach felt unusually forward, and I sensed a quiet discomfort. When I tried to shake the floor manager’s hand, he looked at it but didn’t respond. I didn’t know what to make of it.

A few minutes later, Henrik (HF) invited both of us to sit down. Sahar naturally led the conversation. That’s when I realized she was likely the restaurant manager. But she never introduced herself by title. She clearly wasn’t the owner — which meant she had been placed there by someone else.

During the interview, I asked to be included in the tip sharing, on equal terms with the waitstaff. Sahar replied that she needed to consult with the bar manager and the floor manager. No decision-maker was clearly identified. The responsibility seemed scattered, and no written confirmation followed.

A few days later, the head chef (HF) — who had been present during the original conversation — told me the request had been approved. But I never knew by whom. Whether the approval came from him, from the two managers, or from Sahar, was never clarified.

A financial decision was therefore made in complete opacity: no declared authority, no visible chain of validation, no traceable responsibility.

Layer 2 – Cold Zone: Payment Deadlock

After I left the restaurant, I had no idea when or how I would be paid. No one had ever communicated any payment calendar, contract format, or salary structure. I had never received a payslip, nor any confirmation of how much I was owed.

My first reflex was to contact Henrik — the only figure who had ever introduced himself as a point of reference. I sent him my bank details twice by SMS. He replied once, saying he would forward it to Sahar, and then went silent. The second time, he never responded.

I had no direct contact for Sahar. I had never been given her phone number or professional email. So I started writing to the public reservation address used by customers. After one or two messages, Sahar responded, then quickly shut down the conversation. She said she no longer wished to talk to me by email.

I insisted, as I had no other point of contact. Eventually, she gave me a private address — not to resolve the issue, but to deflect the noise from internal staff. Only after repeated pressure did I receive some information regarding my payslip and issued checks.

In the end, it was Henrik — the founder, who had never spoken to me before — who stepped in. He sent a final email saying the checks had been issued and nothing further could be done. He told me to use the reference numbers and deal with my bank directly. The bank transfer I had asked for was dismissed.

At no point was I proactively informed about my payslip, contract, or payment method. I learned from a colleague on Facebook that payslips were supposed to be produce by the personalkollen app or be accessed online — something I was never told.

The payment had in fact been issued a month earlier, but no one had notified me. Even when I asked repeatedly, I was met with silence, diversion, or avoidance. A structural Cold Zone — nobody responsible, nobody accountable, nobody visible.

Layer 3 – Proxy Authority Drift: Translation as Displacement

During a sensitive moment — likely related to a cleaning shift proposal — Sahar unexpectedly asked a junior waiter, Quentin (26), to act as translator between us. This happened in the middle of the kitchen, in a semi-public context, with no prior notice.

I had never requested translation, nor showed any struggle communicating. I’ve spoken fluent English since 2003, worked in technical support environments, and handled professional translation of documentation. Her own English was limited — making the gesture feel like a symbolic inversion of competence.

The discomfort wasn’t just linguistic. It was contextual. The topic was sensitive, the space was inappropriate, and the presence of a third party — however friendly — turned a private exchange into an infantilizing display of asymmetry.

No consent was asked. No protocol was followed. And no structural explanation was ever given. A private negotiation was repackaged into an impromptu stage act — placing me in a subordinate posture.

Proxy Authority Drift: when a junior, unrelated actor becomes the channel for sensitive communication — not due to necessity, but to displace legitimacy.

Detection of Cold Zones

🧊 Layer 2 — Administrative Cold Zone / Post-Exit Ambiguity

📍 Situation
After resigning informally via SMS on Dec 14, 2024, Patrice received a reply from Henrik Quan on Dec 15 stating that “the managers” had decided to part ways. No formal process was initiated. No contract had ever been signed. No written dismissal, no timeline, no record.

In the following weeks, Patrice attempted multiple times to:

  • Confirm when and how he would be paid
  • Retrieve his salary, payslip, and contract
  • Clarify whether his account details had been registered

Instead, he was told by Henrik to contact Sahar, and then told by Sahar to stop contacting them altogether — with threats of police reports.

Despite providing his bank information on Dec 19 (📄 SMS evidence), payment was processed via Swedbank paper checks sent to an old Stockholm address, without notification, one month later. No apology, no procedural explanation.

🔍 Typology

  • No clear point of contact post-exit
  • Breakdown of internal coordination (Henrik defers to Sahar; Sahar defers to Henrik)
  • Legal ambiguities (no contract, no onboarding record, unregistered payment flow)
  • Communications blocked by emotional escalation ("You are frightening me")

🔗 Traceability & Evidence

  • 📎 Final Reminder to Quan — Jan 26, 2025
  • 📎 Dispute over cheque method — Jan 28, 2025
  • 📎 Termination thread — Dec 14–15, 2024

🧠 Interpretation
This is not a pure financial Cold Zone — it’s a delegated opacity trap, where two actors (Henrik and Sahar) avoid direct responsibility. The employee is legally disconnected but structurally dependent, and no one “owns” the final step. The zone persists until intervention or legal escalation.

“Everyone claims the process is ‘done’, yet no one provides confirmation or restitution.”

1. Dual Authority Without Interlock

Source: Neither Sahar nor Henrik ever introduced themselves with a role. No title was disclosed, no responsibilities outlined. My only pre-application reference was Henrik — publicly visible, with no company named.

Upon arrival, Sahar handled the interview but gave no indication of her status. All staff referred to Henrik as “the boss,” but he remained distant, rarely interacted with team members, and never issued instructions.

Sahar gave operational orders but was present only a few days per week, and never described as a manager. Shift assignments came from Henrik the Head Chef; pay and admin issues were referred to Sahar.

No coordination between them was ever observed. No Slack, no shared documentation, no joint appearance. I never saw them in the same room or interacting.

Their domains — if any — were fragmented, undocumented, and never made visible to staff.

LP/GP Framing: Existence of two hierarchical figures with no interlock reveals a lack of escalation protocol and absence of shared accountability. Risk of conflict mismanagement and diluted authority traceability.

Layer 2 – Vacant Authority Zone: Dishwashing Station

There was no onboarding. No documented routine. No written guidance. The workflow was revealed step-by-step, orally, by different people. Garbage bags, laundry, fry oil, carts, storage — every task came informally, fragmented, and without a defined hierarchy.

From the start, no one claimed authority over the area. Not a single shift coordinator came to check. No checklist, no SOP, no fallback contact. It felt like a zone you enter and improvise your own rules — tolerated, but ungoverned.

The floor manager never addressed me. The head chef never coordinated. And Sahar — while formally overseeing payroll and administrative topics — never engaged with the physical space. I was left to interface with the kitchen indirectly, often crossing into operational gaps unplanned.

The role existed because the site needed it — but the structure denied it. The most revealing part: when I was spontaneously told I would become the lead "the main guy" of the dish zone — a title without mandate, budget, or oversight.

Vacant Authority Zone: when a critical function operates without anchor — no leader, no escalation, no integration — and becomes an orphaned surface for spontaneous projection.

LP/GP Framing

From an LP lens, this is a classic early-warning zone: absence of governance in a high-turnover, high-risk function. Lack of traceability leads to potential compliance exposure (injuries, retention disputes, liability gaps).

From a GP lens, this zone reflects poor structure-design: roles assigned by void, not by protocol. Leadership is reactive. No one owns the function — yet the function exists. Strategic leak. Organizational entropy.

3. Proxy Authority Drift – Quentin as Shadow Coordinator

Source: A non-manager (Quentin) becomes central in explanations, conflict resolution and norm setting, with no official mandate nor compensation.

LP/GP Framing: A textbook proxy authority drift: real power flows toward informal actors in absence of structured governance. Leads to system fragility and HR opacity.

4. Shadow Onboarding – No Contract, Verbal Commitments

Source: Verbal agreement to hire, no contract provided, no follow-up despite repeated confirmations. Lack of written trace, no escalation channel exists.

LP/GP Framing: Structural grey zone. Formal HR commitment deferred indefinitely, placing legal and operational risk onto the employee. Indicates systemic avoidance mechanisms.
