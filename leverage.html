💶 Bank Leverage Strategy — Dual Blueprint Across Two Generations

🏦 Bank Leverage & Proxy Governance

📍2004 – The Founding Blueprint (Isakaya Koi v1)

In July 2004, Henrik Kei Siebert secures a 4 million SEK bank loan with SEB under the company Junitrådet 5 AB. The contract is structured for 5 years and 6 months at an interest rate of 4.75%. To meet the bank’s creditworthiness expectations, Henrik lists himself as Board Member, and strategically appoints his father Erich Karl Siebert (63) as Deputy. A personal guarantee (pantförskrivning) is signed, and the planned ownership is distributed as 90% Henrik and 10% Carl-Johan Östlund.

This structure projects a classic generational configuration: a young entrepreneur backed by a senior figure, signaling to the bank an image of long-term stability and intergenerational accountability. This mirage of security helps Henrik unlock financing that might otherwise be inaccessible.

📍2018 – The Relaunch Under HSSC Event AB

Fourteen years later, a similar strategy is used. In 2018, Saahil Chudasama registers Chudasama Mat & Event AB, joined by Sahar El Nasser as Deputy Board Member. The stated mission is to open a new hospitality venue, supported by a couple structure once again — this time projecting a “reliable household” for bank and regulatory scrutiny.

Shortly after approval, the company is renamed HSSC Event AB — a nod to the joint branding of Henrik Siebert and Saahil Chudasama. An alcohol license is granted, along with a seating plan for 89 guests. The venue operates under a revenue-indexed rental agreement with Elite Hotels, which anchors the business inside a high-credibility address and market context.

Once all permits and infrastructure are secured, control is silently rerouted: 75% of the equity is transferred to KEHDA Holding AB, controlled by Daniella Lin Dahl, and 25% is retained by Saahil Chudasama. Henrik never appears on any public registry during this phase, though his operational influence is known.

This playbook mirrors the 2004 sequence almost point for point — with one difference: instead of a father-son duo, the structure now relies on a partner-partner dynamic. The objective remains constant: secure bank financing via the appearance of a stable couple, while preparing a quiet handover of strategic control once approvals are in place.

🔍 Tactical Analysis

The duplication of this tactic suggests a deeper behavioral pattern. By using “proxy founders” to front licensing and financing procedures, Henrik manages to secure leverage without direct exposure. Once the regulatory gates are passed, real ownership is redirected to familiar holding entities — KEHDA Holding AB and CHUDASAMA Invest AB — thereby insulating the true beneficiaries and diffusing legal visibility.

From a risk governance perspective, this maneuver raises questions. If the true operator is excluded from filings, then accountability becomes diluted. Yet the strategy has proven highly effective: it grants access to alcohol permits, commercial licenses, and credit — all while bypassing personal financial scrutiny. The question now is whether this pattern can continue without increased scrutiny or internal breakdowns.

🧠 Interpretation: Pattern or Coincidence?

This repetition of strategy suggests a deliberate playbook. In both instances, a legally clean figure is placed front-stage to access banking and licensing channels. Once key milestones are reached, financial control is transferred off-record, sometimes to entities that would otherwise have raised concerns.

The consistency in address usage, naming conventions, and rerouting timing strengthens the hypothesis of a conscious workaround. It also reflects a broader logic of proxy-based governance, with real operators shielded behind pre-approved surrogates.
